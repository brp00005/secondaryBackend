Frequently Asked Questions

The Massachusetts Chamber of Commerce is a statewide organization headquartered in Boston at 60 State Street that advocates for pro-business policies and connects employers with resources for growth. The Chamber serves as the voice of business across the Commonwealth, working on legislative and regulatory issues affecting Massachusetts businesses.

Massachusetts has 111 chambers of commerce serving communities across its 14 counties. This includes major metropolitan chambers like the Greater Boston Chamber (which hosts 1,400+ leaders at its Annual Meeting), Worcester Regional Chamber, Springfield Regional Chamber, and Cape Cod Chamber, as well as numerous local city and town chambers throughout the Commonwealth.

The Greater Boston Chamber of Commerce is the convener, voice, and advocate for the region's business community. Located at 75 State Street in Boston, the Chamber supports bills to help small businesses and hosts major events including its Annual Meeting attended by over 1,400 civic, business, and non-profit leaders. The Greater Boston Chamber serves as the primary chamber for the Boston metropolitan area.

Chamber membership costs vary by chamber and business size. Small local chambers may charge $200-500 annually, while larger metropolitan chambers like the Greater Boston Chamber may range from $500-3,000+ depending on company size and membership level. Cape Cod and tourism-focused chambers often have seasonal business rates. Contact your local chamber for current pricing and membership packages.

Yes, Cape Cod has numerous active chambers including the Cape Cod Chamber of Commerce (the regional chamber based in Centerville), Hyannis Area Chamber, Falmouth Chamber, Provincetown Chamber, Chatham Chamber, Orleans Chamber, Dennis Chamber, Yarmouth Chamber, and others serving individual towns. The islands also have chambers: Martha's Vineyard Chamber and Nantucket Island Chamber. These chambers support tourism, hospitality, retail, and local businesses.

Massachusetts has prominent industry associations including Massachusetts Biotechnology Council (MassBio) serving the life sciences industry, Massachusetts High Technology Council, Associated Industries of Massachusetts (AIM), Retailers Association of Massachusetts, Massachusetts Restaurant Association, and Massachusetts Bankers Association. These organizations complement local chambers by providing industry-specific advocacy, resources, and networking.

To find your local chamber: 1) Visit massachusettschamberofcommerce.com for the MA Chamber Directory, 2) Search the U.S. Chamber of Commerce directory at uschamber.com/co/chambers/massachusetts, 3) Search online for "[your city/town name] chamber of commerce Massachusetts," 4) Check visitma.com/chambers-of-commerce for tourism-related chambers. Most Massachusetts cities and larger towns have at least one chamber or business association.

Massachusetts has several diversity organizations including Massachusetts LGBT Chamber, Black Economic Council of Massachusetts, Massachusetts Minority Business Council, Hispanic Chamber of Commerce, Asian American Civic Association, and Women's Business Enterprise Council. These organizations provide specialized networking, certification assistance for supplier diversity programs, and resources for diverse business owners throughout the Commonwealth.

The Cambridge Chamber of Commerce serves Cambridge's diverse business community, from technology startups and biotech companies near MIT and Harvard to local retailers and restaurants. Located at 859 Massachusetts Avenue, the Cambridge Chamber provides networking, advocacy, and business development resources for one of the most innovative cities in the United States.

Massachusetts chambers support small businesses through business development workshops, networking events, advocacy for small business-friendly policies on Beacon Hill, connections to Massachusetts SBDC resources, assistance with permits and licensing, access to financing through MassDevelopment, marketing opportunities, mentorship programs, and connections to larger businesses. The Greater Boston Chamber specifically advocates for bills to help small businesses.
