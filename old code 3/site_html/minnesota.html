Minnesota Chamber of Commerce

Established: 1949 | Members: 2,300+ businesses and organizations

The Minnesota Chamber of Commerce is the unified voice for Minnesota business, representing companies of all sizes and industries across the state. As Minnesota's largest business advocacy organization, the Chamber works to ensure a thriving economy, a competitive business climate, and a prosperous future for all Minnesotans.

Website: mnchamber.com

Key Services

  • Business advocacy and legislative representation
  • Policy development and government affairs
  • Business networking and connections
  • Educational programs and seminars
  • Economic research and data
  • Member benefits and cost savings programs
Legislative Leadership: The Minnesota Chamber serves as the state's leading business advocacy organization, representing member interests at the state capitol, in congressional offices, and in communities across Minnesota. The Chamber's policy priorities focus on workforce development, tax policy, regulatory reform, and economic competitiveness.

Small Business Resources in Minnesota

Minnesota offers extensive support programs and resources for small business owners and entrepreneurs through state agencies, non-profit organizations, and chamber partnerships.

Minnesota Small Business Development Centers (SBDC)

Statewide network providing free, confidential business consulting and affordable training programs. Services include business planning, market research, financial analysis, and access to capital guidance.

Services Offered:

  • One-on-one business consulting
  • Business plan development
  • Financial projections and analysis
  • Marketing strategy development
  • Access to financing resources

Website: mnsbdc.com

Minnesota Department of Employment and Economic Development (DEED)

State agency providing business development assistance, workforce programs, and economic development resources. DEED offers grants, loans, tax incentives, and technical assistance for Minnesota businesses.

Programs Available:

  • Business development grants and loans
  • Export assistance programs
  • Workforce training grants
  • Site location assistance
  • Entrepreneurship resources

Website: mn.gov/deed

Minnesota Business Finance Corporation

Non-profit organization providing access to capital for Minnesota businesses through SBA 504 loans, USDA Business & Industry loans, and other financing programs. Serves businesses unable to obtain conventional financing.

Financing Options:

  • SBA 504 loans for real estate and equipment
  • USDA B&I guaranteed loans
  • Minnesota Small Business Loan Guarantee
  • Working capital loan programs
  • Loan packaging and application support

Website: mbfc.org

Metropolitan Economic Development Association (MEDA)

Non-profit organization providing business development services, lending, and real estate development focused on minority and women-owned businesses in the Twin Cities region.

Support Services:

  • Business consulting and technical assistance
  • Microloans and small business lending
  • Commercial real estate development
  • Contractor development programs
  • Minority business certification assistance

Website: meda.net

Frequently Asked Questions

The Minnesota Chamber of Commerce is the state's largest business advocacy organization, representing over 2,300 businesses and organizations of all sizes and industries across Minnesota. Founded in 1949, the Chamber serves as the unified voice for Minnesota business, advocating for policies that promote economic growth, competitiveness, and job creation.

The Minnesota Chamber works at the state capitol, in congressional offices, and in communities statewide to advance pro-business legislation, oppose burdensome regulations, and create a favorable business climate. The Chamber's headquarters is located at 380 Saint Peter Street in Saint Paul, with President and CEO Doug Loon leading the organization's advocacy and member services efforts.

Minnesota has over 90 local and regional chambers of commerce operating across all 87 counties. These chambers range from large metropolitan organizations like the Minneapolis Regional Chamber (serving approximately 1,200 businesses) and Saint Paul Area Chamber to smaller community chambers serving towns and rural areas throughout the state.

Major chambers include the Duluth Area Chamber (1,100+ members), Rochester Area Chamber, Bloomington Chamber, St. Cloud Area Chamber, and dozens of regional chambers serving Greater Minnesota communities. Each chamber operates independently while often coordinating with the statewide Minnesota Chamber on policy advocacy and member services.

Chamber membership provides multiple benefits including business advocacy representation at state and local government levels, networking opportunities with business leaders and decision-makers, educational programs and workshops on business topics, marketing exposure through member directories and promotional opportunities, and access to cost-saving programs.

Specific benefits often include discounted healthcare and workers' compensation insurance, payment processing savings, member-to-member referral networks, ribbon cutting ceremonies for new businesses, leadership development programs, economic data and market research, and connections to local economic development resources. The Minnesota Chamber also offers HR support services, compliance assistance, and legislative updates to keep members informed of policy changes affecting their businesses.

Membership dues vary by chamber size and member company revenue, typically ranging from $200-$500 annually for small businesses to several thousand dollars for large corporations.

The Minnesota Chamber of Commerce operates at the statewide level, focusing primarily on legislative advocacy, public policy development, and issues affecting businesses across all of Minnesota. The state chamber represents member interests at the Minnesota State Capitol, in congressional offices, and on statewide economic policies.

Local and regional chambers focus on their specific geographic communities, addressing city and county government issues, promoting local economic development, organizing community business events, and connecting businesses within their service areas. Local chambers often work on Main Street revitalization, local zoning issues, community festivals, and regional tourism promotion.

Many businesses join both their local chamber and the Minnesota Chamber to benefit from both community-level networking and statewide advocacy representation. The chambers often coordinate on shared policy priorities while maintaining distinct local and statewide roles.

Minnesota chambers provide extensive small business support through affordable membership rates, educational programs tailored to small business needs, networking events designed for business-to-business connections, and advocacy for policies that reduce regulatory burdens on small employers.

Chambers connect small businesses to resources including the Minnesota Small Business Development Centers (SBDC) network for free consulting, Department of Employment and Economic Development (DEED) programs for grants and loans, and local economic development authorities for site selection and expansion assistance. Many chambers offer mentor programs pairing experienced business owners with new entrepreneurs.

Cost-saving programs specifically benefit small businesses through group purchasing power for healthcare insurance, workers' compensation coverage, payment processing, shipping discounts, and office supplies. Chambers also provide visibility opportunities through ribbon cuttings, member spotlights, and directory listings that help small businesses gain customers and credibility in their communities.

Chambers of commerce serve as key partners in regional economic development efforts, working closely with local and county economic development authorities (EDAs), DEED regional offices, and Workforce Development Boards. Chambers help recruit new businesses, support existing business expansions, promote their regions to site selectors, and advocate for infrastructure improvements that enhance business competitiveness.

The Minnesota Chamber focuses on statewide economic competitiveness through tax policy advocacy, workforce development initiatives, regulatory reform, and promotion of Minnesota's business advantages. Regional chambers often lead downtown revitalization projects, coordinate business retention programs, conduct economic impact studies, and develop strategic plans for community growth.

Chambers also support entrepreneurship through connections to innovation hubs, incubators, and accelerators. In Minnesota's diverse economy spanning agriculture, manufacturing, healthcare, technology, and tourism, chambers provide industry-specific support and help businesses access capital, workforce, and markets necessary for growth.

Start by identifying chambers serving your business location. Most businesses join their city or county chamber based on their primary business address. Research chambers in your area by searching online for "[Your City] Minnesota Chamber of Commerce" or visiting the Minnesota Chamber website for a directory of local chambers.

Consider chamber size, membership demographics, and programs offered. Larger chambers typically offer more networking events, extensive educational programs, and stronger government advocacy, while smaller chambers may provide more intimate networking and closer community connections. Review chamber websites to see member lists, event calendars, and policy priorities to ensure alignment with your business interests.

Attend chamber events as a guest before joining to evaluate member engagement, event quality, and organizational professionalism. Many chambers offer trial memberships or allow non-members to attend initial events. Contact chamber staff to discuss membership benefits specific to your industry and business size. Businesses with multiple locations often join chambers in each community they serve, while also considering Minnesota Chamber membership for statewide advocacy representation.

The Minnesota Chamber focuses on policy priorities including workforce development and education to address talent shortages, tax policy reform to maintain competitive business tax rates, regulatory reform to reduce compliance burdens, healthcare affordability and accessibility, transportation infrastructure investment, and energy policy that balances costs with environmental goals.

Local chambers advocate on community-specific issues such as local tax levies, zoning and land use policies, parking and transportation access, economic development incentives, and municipal regulations affecting business operations. Chambers often testify at city council meetings, county board sessions, and state legislative hearings to represent business perspectives.

Recent advocacy efforts have addressed paid leave legislation, minimum wage policies, unemployment insurance reform, workers' compensation system improvements, and small business tax relief. Chambers also support workforce training programs, K-12 education standards aligned with career readiness, and immigration policies that allow businesses to access needed talent.

Minnesota chambers host diverse networking events including monthly business mixers and after-hours socials, industry-specific roundtables bringing together businesses in similar sectors, leadership programs developing emerging business leaders, women in business groups, young professionals networks, and annual meetings featuring prominent speakers.

Specialized networking includes business expos and trade shows, golf outings and sporting events, luncheon speaker series featuring business experts and legislators, ribbon cutting ceremonies celebrating business openings and expansions, business showcase events where members promote their products and services, and speed networking sessions for rapid connections.

Many chambers organize committee meetings focused on government affairs, economic development, education, and community issues, providing opportunities to work alongside other business leaders on shared priorities. Regional chambers also coordinate multi-chamber events bringing together businesses from across multiple communities for larger networking opportunities.

Minnesota chambers operate as non-profit organizations funded primarily through member dues, which typically account for 60-80% of annual revenue. Dues are structured using tiered pricing based on company size, number of employees, or annual revenue, ensuring businesses of all sizes can participate. Small businesses may pay $200-$500 annually, mid-sized companies $500-$2,500, and large corporations several thousand dollars or more.

Additional revenue sources include event sponsorships from member businesses, registration fees for educational programs and special events, advertising in chamber publications and directories, and program income from cost-saving member benefit programs. Some chambers receive grants from local governments or economic development organizations for specific projects.

The Minnesota Chamber also generates revenue through its educational conferences, certification programs, member services including HR support and insurance programs, and corporate partnerships. Chambers maintain 501(c)(6) tax-exempt status as business leagues, though membership dues are generally tax-deductible as ordinary business expenses (subject to certain limitations under tax law).
